## Overview

Jörmungandr (Old Norse Jǫrmungandr; also Miðgarðsormr, the 'Midgard Serpent') is the colossal serpent of Norse mythology that lies beneath the sea encircling Midgard, the world of humans, biting his own tail in an ouroboros configuration. Snorri Sturluson's Prose Edda (compiled c. 1220) and the Eddic poems of the Codex Regius (c. 1270) preserve the canonical narratives. Jörmungandr is the middle child of the trickster god Loki and the jötunn Angrboða; his siblings are the wolf Fenrir and the underworld ruler Hel. According to Snorri, the All-Father Odin cast the young serpent into the encircling ocean, where he grew so large as to bite his own tail. Three myths dominate: (1) the fishing trip with the giant Hymir, in which Thor uses an ox-head as bait, hooks the serpent, and is on the verge of striking it with Mjǫllnir before Hymir cuts the line in terror (preserved in Hymiskviða and the Prose Edda; depicted on the Altuna Runestone, the Gosforth fishing-stone, the Hørdum stone, and the Ardre VIII picture stone); (2) the Útgarða-Loki episode, in which Thor is challenged to lift a 'cat' that turns out to be the serpent in disguise — Thor manages to raise one of its paws, frightening Útgarða-Loki and his court; and (3) Ragnarök, when the serpent rises from the sea, poisons the air, and meets Thor in single combat: Thor slays Jörmungandr but staggers nine paces and falls dead from the venom. Jörmungandr is thus a cosmic boundary-being whose movement signals the end of the world.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Old Norse Jǫrmungandr, from jǫrmun- ('huge, vast, mighty') + gandr ('staff', 'wand', 'monster', often glossed as 'magical staff' or 'monster'); the name combines 'huge' with the same gandr seen in Vánagandr (one of Fenrir's bynames) and gandreið ('witch's ride')
- **Meaning**: 'Huge monster' or 'great staff'; the alternative name Miðgarðsormr means 'Midgard Serpent'
- **First use**: Attested in Old Norse poetry (e.g. Völuspá, Hymiskviða); systematized in Snorri Sturluson's Prose Edda (c. 1220)

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Indo-European chaos-serpent comparative motif** _(Calvert Watkins (How to Kill a Dragon, 1995), Bruce Lincoln)_

Comparativists since Bruce Lincoln and Calvert Watkins have argued that the Thor / Jörmungandr combat is a Norse reflex of a deep Indo-European storm-god vs serpent / dragon-slaying mythologem (cf. Indra / Vritra, Tarhunt / Illuyanka, Apollo / Python).

### Skeptical

**Strictly mythological** _(Mainstream Norse-studies scholarship)_

There is no claim that Jörmungandr is a cryptid or living being; he is a mythological figure preserved in Eddic poetry, skaldic verse, and Viking-Age stone art.
